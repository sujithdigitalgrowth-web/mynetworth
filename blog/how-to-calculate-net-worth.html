How to Calculate Your Net Worth in India

April 15, 2026 · 8 min read

If someone asked you "how much are you worth financially?" right now, could you answer confidently? Most people can't. They know their salary, maybe their bank balance, but not their complete financial picture. That's where net worth comes in.

Your net worth is the single best number to understand your financial health. It's not about how much you earn — it's about how much you've kept and grown. Let's break down exactly how to calculate it.

The Formula

Net worth is beautifully simple:

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

That's it. Add up everything you own (assets), subtract everything you owe (liabilities), and the result is your net worth. Let's go deeper into each side.

Step 1: List All Your Assets

Assets are anything you own that has monetary value. For an Indian household, this typically includes:

Liquid Assets (easily accessible)

  • Savings accounts — all bank accounts combined
  • Fixed deposits — current value including accrued interest
  • Liquid mutual funds — current NAV value
  • Cash in hand — including digital wallets

Investments

  • Mutual funds — equity, debt, hybrid (check current value on AMC app)
  • Stocks — current market value of your demat holdings
  • PPF/EPF — check balance on EPFO portal or passbook
  • NPS — current value from NPS app
  • Sukanya Samriddhi, KVP, NSC — current maturity value
  • Gold/Silver — physical gold, sovereign gold bonds, digital gold at today's price

Property

  • Real estate — estimated current market value (not purchase price)
  • Land — current circle rate or market rate

Other Assets

  • Vehicle — current resale value (check OLX/CarDekho for estimates)
  • Business value — if you own a business, its estimated worth
  • Lending — money you've lent to others that you expect to get back

Tip: Use current market values, not what you paid. Your home may be worth more (or less) than you bought it for. Stocks fluctuate daily. Use today's value.

Step 2: List All Your Liabilities

Liabilities are everything you owe. The outstanding balance, not the original loan amount.

  • Home loan — current outstanding principal
  • Car loan — remaining balance
  • Personal loan — outstanding amount
  • Gold loan — current balance
  • Education loan — remaining principal
  • Credit card debt — total outstanding (not just minimum due)
  • Borrowed from family/friends — if you genuinely plan to repay it
  • Any EMI purchases — phone, laptop, appliance EMIs remaining

Step 3: Calculate

Let's look at an example for a 32-year-old IT professional in Bangalore:

Assets:
Savings account: ₹2,50,000
Fixed deposits: ₹5,00,000
Mutual funds (SIPs): ₹8,40,000
Stocks: ₹3,20,000
EPF balance: ₹6,80,000
PPF: ₹4,50,000
Gold (wife's jewellery): ₹3,00,000
Car (resale value): ₹4,50,000
Total Assets: ₹37,90,000

Liabilities:
Car loan outstanding: ₹2,80,000
Credit card: ₹45,000
Total Liabilities: ₹3,25,000

Net Worth = ₹37,90,000 − ₹3,25,000 = ₹34,65,000

This person's net worth is about ₹34.65 lakhs. Not bad for 32! But the number itself is less important than what happens next — tracking it over time.

Step 4: Track It Monthly

Calculating your net worth once is useful. Tracking it every month is transformative. Here's why:

  • You see if your wealth is actually growing, shrinking, or stagnant
  • It motivates you — watching the number go up is addicting
  • It catches problems early — if net worth drops, you investigate why
  • It shifts your financial mindset permanently

Set a calendar reminder on the 1st of every month. Update your asset values (mutual fund NAVs, stock portfolio, loan balances). It takes 10 minutes and gives you financial clarity that most people never have.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Don't include household items — your TV, furniture, and clothes don't count. They have almost zero resale value.
  • Don't use purchase price for property — use current market value. Check 99acres, MagicBricks, or ask a local agent.
  • Don't forget EPF — it's real money, just locked up. Include it.
  • Don't ignore small debts — that ₹30,000 credit card balance counts.
  • Don't compare with others — different people are at different stages. Compare with your past self.

Net Worth Benchmarks by Age (India)

These are rough guidelines based on annual income:

Age 25: 0.5–1x annual income
Age 30: 1–3x annual income
Age 35: 3–5x annual income
Age 40: 5–8x annual income
Age 45: 8–12x annual income
Age 50: 12–15x annual income

If you're ahead — great. If you're behind — you now know, and you can fix it.
