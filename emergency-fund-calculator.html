📖 What is an Emergency Fund?

An emergency fund is a cash reserve specifically set aside for unplanned expenses or financial emergencies. Think of it as your financial safety net — it catches you when life throws surprises.

Examples of emergencies: job loss, medical bills, urgent home or car repairs, family emergencies, or any situation where you suddenly need money you didn't budget for.

The key rule: an emergency fund should be in a liquid account — savings bank, liquid mutual fund, or sweep FD. Not in stocks, not in property, not locked in a 5-year FD. You need to access it within 24 hours.

💡 How Much Emergency Fund Do You Need?

The standard advice is 3–6 months of expenses. But the right number depends on your situation:

3 months — You have a stable government/corporate job, dual income household, no dependents

6 months — Standard recommendation for most salaried professionals with a family

9 months — Single income household, or you work in a volatile industry (startups, sales)

12 months — Self-employed, freelancer, business owner, or sole earner with high responsibilities

When in doubt, aim for 6 months. You can always increase it later.

❓ FAQ

Should I invest my emergency fund?
No — not in stocks or equity mutual funds. The whole point is instant access with no risk. Keep it in a savings account or liquid mutual fund. Returns don't matter here; availability does.
Should I build emergency fund or pay off debt first?
Build a small emergency fund first (1–2 months), then aggressively pay off high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans). Once debt is cleared, build the full 6-month fund.
Does FD count as an emergency fund?
A sweep FD or short-term FD (with no penalty for early withdrawal) can work. But a regular locked FD with penalties is not ideal — you need money available within 24 hours without losing returns.
